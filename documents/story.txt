# The City Beneath the Sand

For three hundred years, the desert of Kharad had swallowed armies, kingdoms, caravans, and dreams.

People who lived along its edges spoke of the desert the way sailors spoke of the sea—with fear disguised as respect. Sandstorms rose without warning. Entire villages disappeared beneath dunes overnight. Travelers heard voices after sunset, whispers carried through the wind in languages no living person understood.

And somewhere deep beneath those endless golden wastes, according to legend, slept a city built before history itself.

Most believed it was a myth.

Arin Vale did not.

---

Arin was twenty-three years old when he crossed the Gate of Ash and entered the desert alone.

He carried a rusted compass that no longer pointed north, a leather map stitched with symbols instead of roads, and a promise he had made to his dying father.

“Find what they buried,” his father had whispered. “Before the Order does.”

Those had been his final words.

For years Arin had searched libraries, ruins, and forgotten temples for clues about the lost city called Nareth. Every story contradicted the next. Some said it had been destroyed by war. Others claimed its people vanished in a single night.

But one detail remained constant:

Nareth possessed the Heart Engine—a machine capable of bending time itself.

The Empire wanted it.

The Order of Glass wanted it more.

And if either found it first, the world would never survive.

---

By the fifth day in the desert, Arin’s water was nearly gone.

Heat shimmered across the dunes like ghostly oceans. His boots sank into burning sand with every step. The wind carved sharp patterns across the landscape, erasing all tracks behind him.

He checked the strange compass again.

The needle spun wildly before jerking east.

Always east.

At sunset, he saw lights in the distance.

Not campfires.

Blue lights.

Moving.

Arin climbed a dune carefully and peered down.

A caravan crawled across the sand below—six black wagons surrounded by armored riders carrying silver spears. Lanterns glowing blue hung from each wagon.

Order soldiers.

Arin cursed under his breath and ducked lower.

The Order of Glass served no king. They answered only to the High Seers, scholars who believed knowledge belonged solely to the powerful. Entire cities feared them.

If they were here, they were close to Nareth too.

One rider separated from the caravan and headed directly toward Arin’s dune.

His pulse quickened.

The rider stopped below.

“You can come down,” a woman’s voice called. “Or fall trying to run.”

Arin slowly stood.

The rider removed her hood.

Dark hair. Bronze skin. A scar across one eyebrow. Calm gray eyes that looked far too observant.

“You’ve been following our route for two days,” she said.

“I haven’t.”

“You hide badly.”

Arin considered lying again, then gave up.

“What does the Order want in the eastern dunes?”

Her gaze sharpened instantly.

“So you do know something.”

The woman dismounted smoothly.

“My name is Lyra.”

“Arin.”

“Treasure hunter?”

“Scholar.”

“Those are usually the same thing with better clothing.”

Despite himself, Arin almost smiled.

Almost.

Lyra studied him another moment.

“You should leave the desert,” she said quietly. “Something is waking beneath it.”

Before Arin could answer, the ground trembled.

Both froze.

A low sound rolled through the dunes—not thunder, not wind.

A roar.

The sand behind the caravan exploded upward.

A creature burst from beneath the earth.

Forty feet long.

Covered in black scales.

Its mouth opened into rings of jagged teeth.

The caravan erupted into chaos.

“Sand wyrm!” someone screamed.

The monster crashed through a wagon, splintering wood instantly. Horses shrieked. Soldiers scattered.

Lyra drew twin curved blades from her back.

Arin stared.

“You’re attacking that?”

“You have a better idea?”

The wyrm lunged toward fleeing riders.

Without thinking, Arin grabbed a flare from his pack and struck it against stone. Crimson fire burst alive.

The creature turned sharply toward him.

“Terrible idea,” Lyra muttered.

The wyrm charged.

Arin ran.

Sand flew beneath his boots as the beast thundered after him. He could feel heat from its breath.

“Left!” Lyra shouted.

He dove sideways just as she leapt onto the creature’s back.

Her blades slammed between scales.

The wyrm shrieked violently.

Arin rolled down a dune, coughing sand from his lungs. Above him, Lyra clung to the monster while it twisted through the desert like a living storm.

Then Arin’s compass began glowing.

Bright gold.

The sand beneath the wyrm collapsed.

A massive stone structure erupted upward from beneath the dunes.

Ancient towers.

Broken bridges.

Black walls covered in silver markings.

The lost city of Nareth had awakened.

The wyrm disappeared into the collapsing sands with a final scream.

Silence fell.

Every surviving soldier stared at the city in horror.

Arin stood frozen.

After years of searching, impossible stories, dead ends, and failures—

He had found it.

---

The gates of Nareth opened by themselves.

No chains moved.

No gears turned.

The doors simply drifted apart silently, as if the city had been waiting.

Cold air flowed outward carrying the scent of rain and old metal.

Lyra approached carefully.

“This place should not exist,” she whispered.

Inside the walls stood enormous buildings carved from black stone that reflected light like mirrors. Strange crystals floated above empty streets. Statues lined the avenues—thousands of them.

Not statues, Arin realized.

People.

Turned to stone.

Men, women, children.

All frozen where they stood.

Some looked terrified.

Others looked upward peacefully.

At the center of the city rose a tower so tall its peak vanished into darkness overhead.

“The Heart Engine is there,” Arin said softly.

A voice echoed behind them.

“Yes.”

Order soldiers surrounded them from every side.

And at their center stood an old man in white robes carrying a staff of glass.

High Seer Malrec.

Arin’s blood ran cold.

Malrec smiled kindly, though his eyes held no warmth.

“You’ve done excellent work finding the city for us.”

Lyra stepped subtly closer to Arin.

“You followed me,” he realized.

“Of course,” Malrec replied. “Your father was brilliant. Predictable, but brilliant.”

Arin clenched his fists.

“You killed him.”

Malrec tilted his head slightly.

“He refused to surrender knowledge that belonged to humanity.”

“You mean to you.”

“Power in lesser hands creates chaos.”

The old man looked toward the great tower.

“Tonight,” he whispered, almost reverently, “history begins again.”

Soldiers seized Arin and Lyra before they could fight back.

They were dragged through the silent city toward the tower.

As they passed the stone figures, Arin noticed something horrifying.

Their expressions were changing.

Tiny cracks spread across their bodies.

Eyes slowly turning toward the travelers.

Watching them.

---

The tower doors closed behind the prisoners with a deafening boom.

Inside, machinery unlike anything Arin had ever imagined stretched endlessly upward—massive rings of silver rotating through empty air, streams of glowing energy moving between them like rivers of light.

At the center floated a crystal sphere the size of a house.

The Heart Engine.

Malrec approached it with trembling awe.

“Do you understand what this means?” he asked softly. “Time itself. We could erase wars. Prevent empires from falling. Reshape civilization.”

“You’ll destroy everything,” Arin said.

Malrec smiled sadly.

“All great change appears destructive at first.”

He placed his hand against the crystal.

The tower shook violently.

The Engine awakened.

Light exploded across the chamber.

And suddenly Arin saw impossible things.

Cities burning.

Oceans swallowing continents.

The moon split in half.

A shadow stretching across the stars.

Then another vision—

Nareth alive.

Crowded streets. Music. Gardens.

And at the center of the city stood people gathered around the Heart Engine in terror.

One voice echoed through the vision:

“It cannot be controlled.”

The city vanished in white fire.

Arin staggered back to reality.

The Engine wasn’t a weapon.

It was a prison.

Something inside it was trying to escape.

Cracks spread across the crystal sphere.

Darkness leaked through them like smoke.

Malrec stared in horror.

“No…”

The shadows gathered into a shape taller than any human.

A face without eyes.

A voice without sound.

Every statue in the city screamed simultaneously.

The entity spoke directly into their minds.

**YOU OPENED THE DOOR.**

Soldiers collapsed instantly, clutching their heads.

Lyra cut Arin free.

“We need to move now!”

The shadow expanded rapidly across the chamber walls.

Malrec stumbled backward.

“What are you?”

The entity turned toward him.

**THE END OF MEMORY.**

It touched him.

Malrec vanished.

Not killed.

Erased.

One moment he existed.

The next, no one could even remember his face.

Panic spread through the remaining soldiers.

The tower began collapsing.

Arin looked at the Heart Engine and understood the terrible truth.

Nareth’s people had sacrificed themselves to imprison the creature centuries ago.

If the Engine failed completely, the world outside would disappear piece by piece until nothing remained.

Lyra grabbed his arm.

“Tell me you have a plan.”

Arin looked at the glowing compass in his hand.

His father’s compass.

It no longer spun.

It pointed directly toward the Engine.

And suddenly he understood why his father had sent him here.

The compass wasn’t guiding him to the city.

It was the key.

Arin took a long breath.

“If I’m right,” he said quietly, “this may trap it again.”

“And if you’re wrong?”

He looked at the growing darkness consuming the tower.

“Then nobody will remember we existed.”

Lyra stared at him for one long second.

Then she handed him one of her blades.

“For luck.”

Arin stepped toward the Engine.

The entity screamed inside his mind.

**DO NOT BIND ME AGAIN.**

He slammed the compass into the crystal sphere.

Light erupted across the tower.

The entire city shook.

The floating rings accelerated wildly, spinning faster and faster until they became circles of pure white fire.

The shadow lunged.

Arin thought of his father.

Of the desert.

Of every forgotten story buried beneath the sands.

And he twisted the compass.

The world exploded into light.

---

When Arin opened his eyes, dawn was breaking across the desert.

The city of Nareth was gone.

Only dunes remained.

Lyra lay nearby unconscious but breathing.

Arin sat up slowly.

His compass had turned to ash.

For a moment he wondered if everything had been a dream.

Then he noticed something strange.

The desert wind no longer whispered.

The silence felt… peaceful.

Lyra opened her eyes and groaned.

“Please tell me we saved the world.”

“I think so.”

“You sound uncertain.”

“I’m hoping uncertainty is a good sign.”

She laughed weakly.

Then stopped.

“Arin…”

Far in the distance, beyond the dunes, black shapes rose against the morning sky.

Towers.

Huge ones.

Slowly emerging from beneath the sand.

Not one city.

Many.

Arin stared silently as ancient structures awakened across the horizon.

An entire civilization buried beneath the desert.

Waiting.

And somewhere deep below the earth—

Something moved.
